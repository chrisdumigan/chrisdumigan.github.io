Originally written for solo guitar, this work was later expanded for two guitars and was my ﬁrst attempt at this combination. The work is in four movements and is an exercise in an unusual form of variation, or rather one of stark contrasts laid side by side with one another. Therefore each of the movements has two parts, the second of which is a variation of the ﬁrst, but which is almost an opposite of it. The suite gets its name from a style of painting wherein the artist uses such violent contrasts in his canvas.

This work is dedicated to my friend Anne MacCarthy who was the first player to read through this with me, and with whom I gave the first unofficial performance at a Manchester Guitar Circle evening many moons ago.

Chris Dumigan, 2003

Fantasy on "Shenandoah" for solo guitar

Shenandoah has long been one of my very favourite folk/traditional tunes, and so although I considered arranging it in some way for guitar long ago, I hesitated doing so, because I really wanted to do it justice. Part of the problem was the fact the tune had only one refrain and I was mindful of the old saying that you can do nothing to a folk tune except keep repeating it faster and faster. It was only when I considered providing my own opening melody, by way of an introduction to Shenandoah, did the piece really start developing in my mind.

So here you get an introduction of my own prefacing the actual melody of Shenandoah, followed by a return to the introduction, now acting as a link to the final and slightly varied repeat of the folk melody. Throughout I have used open strings clashing with arpeggiated chords, often in high positions, to create a particular effect that I hope players will find evocative and haunting.

Chris Dumigan 2006

From Darkness Into Light for solo guitar

Haus Trekel

×

£12.00

Level: Intermediate

Product Code: Dam 199

Buy from Chris (email)

Buy from Haus Trekel

The two- movement structure encompasses a journey from death to life. The first movement is a Lament for those we have loved and lost. It is grief-stricken, and inconsolable until the moment about halfway through the first movement, when our hero has a sudden happy memory of the person or persons lost. The memories get happier and more excited as for a brief moment he forgets his grief and relives the happy time again in his mind, until... suddenly he remembers, and at the happiest moment he is plunged back into the present, and the piece returns to its sombre beginnings.

The second and final movement, in direct contrast to the first is a celebration of the lives to come. After a short linking introduction, which plays around with what is going to be the main theme of the movement proper, one finds oneself in the middle of a huge street party, a celebration to end all celebrations. Imagine for a second an impossible situation wherein the rules of life and death have for a day been suspended and turned inside out. Everybody in the party has been told that they are going to meet their descendants, all the family members due to be born centuries into the future. The whole mood is one of excitement and joy as the party goers await their future family. Then... from the far distance... a procession can be heard and as it gets closer and closer they realise that it is the descendants making their grand entrance. As the procession meets up with the party revellers, everything ends in happiness and optimism.

Chris Dumigan, July 2008

A Garland of Grainger for solo guitar

Haus Trekel

×

£9.00

Level: Intermediate

Product Code: Dam 206

Buy from Chris (email)

Buy from Haus Trekel

I wrote these free adaptations of three folk songs, after becoming captivated by a BBC Radio 3 broadcast on the works of Percy Grainger, and particularly his association with, and collection of, folk songs around the turn of the century with the aid of wax cylinder recording equipment. Having loved folk songs for as much as thirty years, I was completely unprepared for the recording made by Grainger in 1908 of Joseph Taylor, a 70 year old Lincolnshire peasant folk singer, singing only two verses he remembered of Brigg Fair. I had no idea how the folk singers had sounded before the broadcast and the shock wave it sent through me, immediately sent me into a buzz of compositional activity.

Brigg Fair was therefore composed first on 9/3/97 and begins and ends with an unharmonised rendition of the melody - the same length and with the same mannerisms that Joseph had used. In between, the tune, or the harmonic structure of it, is always there in the form of a fluid variation which gathers momentum throughout. I then sandwiched it with two more pieces Grainger collected in the field.

Lisbon, composed last on 16/3/97 made me think of a processional which begins the piece and records into the distance at the end.

Green Bushes composed on 14/3/97, sounded very dance-like to me and so I have attempted to bring out those characteristics in it.

The set can and should be played without a pause, as the endings of the first two movements link directly with the beginning of the next.

Throughout, I have produced no ultra modern harmonies, but rather tried to remain true to the basic folk nature of the pieces. They are my tribute to Grainger and his pioneering work, but are in no way his harmonisations - in fact, for from it.

The set is respectfully dedicated to Paul Gregory, who gave the first performance at the Westgate Chapel in Lewes on 6/5/97.

Chris Dumigan, December 1997

This Sonata was written in April 1974 and is in 3 movements, but was originally conceived in four, the omitted movement becoming my Intermezzo, published separately. This six-movement suite started out life as a suite for 10-course Renaissance lute, completed in September 1985 and originally requested by a friend who wanted some modern lute music for a project he was about to start. This was my first set of pieces for the classical guitar, which were completed on 15th December 1973. Intermezzo for solo guitar
This little single movement piece was written in April 1974 and began life as one of the middle two movements in my Sonata for solo guitar (published separately), until I realised that stylistically it did not quite fit in with that work. Therefore I decided that the movement was too good to lose completely and hope the players amongst you will enjoy having a go at!

A Fireside Scene
This little single movement was written in March 1974 and started out life as my trying to write a piece of programme music. So I imagined a grandfather in front of a warm fire preparing to read an exciting and magical tale to his grandson (or granddaughter), which is the opening 20 bars which acts as a scene-setting type of idea. Then... we’re off! and he begins his magical tale with his excited grandchild listening with wide eyes to his fantastic story. This is the Allegretto section beginning at bar 21 and the main body of the piece. One can hear little remnants of Russian/Arabian music in the manner of Scheherazade in some of the harmonies which might give the listener/player a little clue as to what the story might be about! The slow introduction then returns and the whole story obviously finishes off in a mysterious stillness with a little ‘ping’ on a high harmonic.

This piece started out as just my version of 'She Moved Through the Fair', without the variations tag, as I had known the melody for several years and yet had not actually committed an arrangement to paper. I arrived to the coda of what is now bar 23, before I realised that my next idea was moving away from the actual melody. Lifedance for three guitars

Lathkill Music (11 pages plus separate parts)

×

£9.00

Level: Advanced

Product Code: LMP025

Buy from Chris (email)

Buy from Lathkill Music

This piece was written at the request of Paul Gregory for his trio, Triptico, to play. It was completed on 30th July 1997 and premiered by Triptico on May 13th 1998 at Brighton College during the Brighton Festival.

The title Lifedance had been with me for a while, as Dance of Life, but nothing happened until I came to write this piece. It is in two joined sections, the first of which begins very mysteriously with a deliberately clashing idea, which re-occurs throughout the first section. It attempts to describe the struggle for life, in all its’ forms, even perhaps primeval life, hence the references to ‘like a groan’ and ‘slithering’. The ‘struggle’ is hard fought, as can be witnessed by the various climaxes and subsequent relapses, until the final climax on the huge E major ‘chord that brings the section to an end, but not without the underlying musical crunches that still occur under the main action right to the end. The second section concerns itself with the joy of life, and is basically a dance of optimism. So therefore the two parts represent the negative and the positive sides of life, one the mirror image of the other, but being the eternal optimist that I am, the good wins through at the end.

This work is respectfully dedicated to Triptico, who are Paul Gregory, Alison Parratt and Brian Ashworth, with grateful thanks.

Chris Dumigan, 2000

Sonatina for two guitars

Lathkill Music (Full score and separate parts)

×

£10.00

Level: Intermediate

Product Code: LMP044

Buy from Lathkill Music

The Sonatina for two guitars is my second work written for this medium. It is very English in style and begins with an introductory theme that pervades the whole work, returning at the heart of the climax of the ﬁnal movement. The first movement goes through many emotions, but begins with a four part textured theme that might remind one of the hustle and bustle of some baroque works, before becoming in succession, more dance-like (its' second theme) and more lyrical (the second theme's continuation). A return to the first theme brings on a mysterious rocking theme which takes the place of the development section but is in itself considerably developed before heralding a climactic and much changed return to the introduction. A final play through the first theme leads into the sudden rush of the coda.

The second movement is in two main parts, the first a haunting folk-like minor key melody in 5/4 and the second an exotically harmonised major key idea. The final movement is a gig-like dance in 12/8 and 15/8 with a very optimistic feel. and has a haunting, enigmatic section in the middle of the movement which hopefully catches the listener unawares. A final return to the dance leads directly to a climactic return to the introduction of the first movement. which in turn becomes the final coda.

The duration is approximately 17 minutes and the first performance was given by Ann McCarthy and myself at the Manchester Guitar Circle (England) in 1996.

Chris Dumigan, 2001

A Waltz Trilogy for two guitars

Lathkill Music (21 pages plus separate parts)

×

£9.00

Level: Advanced

Product Code: LMP024

Buy from Lathkill Music

I have always loved the Grand Waltz, as composed by Tchaikovsky and many others too numerous to mention, and had it in the back of my mind to one day compose something of that order on the guitar. When I am away from home, I take manuscript paper to sketch ideas down, without the guitar in hand. One day the first couple of pages of the first movement appeared without my having any idea what I was doing with it. So once the deed was done, I carried on to produce what you are now looking at. It is a tribute to the waltz in all its’ forms and many variations. So in the ﬁrst movement you get a slightly Latin-American feel to the first idea, with its’ cross rhythms and chromaticisms. In the second movement you get the slow romantic waltz, as a first theme, with a Chopinesque idea as the centre. For the last movement you get the slightly Tchaikovskian idea that is the first theme before a little of Dumigan appears at bar 81 in that rather English sounding section. Having said all that, the idea was to produce a melodic set which would be fun to play and listen to, and which doffed its’ cap towards previous waltzes and their composers. I hope to that extent that I have achieved this. This set was first performed by The Guitar Duo — Paul Gregory and Alison Parratt at Westgate Chapel, Lewes on 5th April 2000 and as such is respectfully dedicated to them both with my grateful thanks.

Chris Dumigan, 2000

Quintet for two guitars, flute, viola and 'cello

Haus Trekel

×

£52.00

Level: Advanced

Product Code: Dam 231

Buy from Chris (email)

Buy from Haus Trekel

The quintet is set in five movements and was completed in March 2002. Considering it takes more than 30 minutes to play it was written on a whim! I had previously written works for all combinations of the instruments in this Quintet, but never put them all together in one work, so I set out to do just that. To my knowledge it has never been performed as yet.

After the sunny disposition of the opening movement, the Scherzo is almost manic, with a deliberately heavily accented rhythm. There is then a contrasting trio section before the opening Scherzo returns. The middle movement, Adagio, is mournful and desolate and a huge contrast to what has gone before. The Waltz that follows is never the friendly dance you might be expecting but rather a wild swirling nightmare reminiscent of a carousel ride gone wrong. The joyful Rondo - Finale has some interesting time signatures and cross rhythms and is both effervescent and optimistic. In the very final moments of the piece, the opening melody from the first movement enters to close the work fortissimo.

Chris Dumigan, 2016
